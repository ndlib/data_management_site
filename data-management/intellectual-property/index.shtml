Management of Intellectual Property

Patents, copyrights and other forms of intellectual property deriving from University Research are managed under the University's Intellectual Property policy. This and other University policies can be found at policy.nd.edu.

In general, when publishing or sharing data that you produced/collected yourself:

  • U.S. factual data is not copyrightable and is in the public domain (yet a particular expression of data can be copyrightable, such as a chart or table in a book)*.
  • However, data can and often is licensed. A license agreement is a legal instrument which indicates in advance under which circumstances your research may be used. You can apply licenses that expand or limit how your data can be used.
  • Licenses and Legal tools can be used to make data and databases freely available. If you want to promote sharing and unlimited use of your data, you can make your data available under a sharing license ( like a Creative Commons a CC0 Declaration) to make this explicit.
  • If you want to make your data available in a restricted, limited-access mannerto protect the privacy of participants in a study or guide downstream uses of the data (e.g., requiring attribution or forbidding for-profit use), you may use more stringent licensing Creative Commons criteria
  • The ICPSR DSDR web toolprovides data producers with suggestions of elements to be considered for inclusion in contracts to disseminate data that are of a confidential or sensitive nature.
  • Read the Office of Research Intellectual Property Protection articlefor more information about the various types of intellectual property most likelyto be encountered in the academic setting.

For more information: The University of Notre Dame has adopted several policies related to the creation and use of copyrighted materials. See Copyright Mattersfor more information. If you are uncertain as to your rights to disseminate data, University of Notre Dame researchers are encouraged to contact the Research Compliance program.

Ethical Conduct

The Office of Research Integrity at the US Dept of HHS provides Guidelines for Responsible Data Management in Scientific Research

The National Academy of Engineering’s Online Ethics Center includes a discussion of Responsible Collection, Retention, Sharing, and Interpretation of Data.
The University of Notre Dame’s Research Integrity Policyaims to foster a research environment that: promotes the responsible conduct of research, research training and any activities related to that research or research training; discourages research misconduct; and deals promptly with allegations or evidence of possible research misconduct.

ND’s Research Compliance Programprovides information, support, expertise, and systems of administration needed to meet the laws, rules, and policies governing research in the most efficient and effective way.

Confidentiality and Human Research:
It is vital to maintain the confidentiality of research subjects for reasons of ethics and to ensure the continuing participation in research. At the same time, data on research subjects can be shared if proper steps are taken to maintain participant confidentiality.

  • Evaluate the sensitivity of your data: Researchers should consider whether or not their data contains either direct or indirect identifiers that could be utilized with other public information to identify research participants. If so, steps should be taken to remove or mask these in public-use data files.
  • Comply with University policy: University of Notre Dame researchers concerned about confidentiality issues with their data should consult the Office of Research’s Human Subjects Research web pages.
  • Familiarize yourself with Federal Dept of Health and Human Services’ Human Subject Research policies and resources.

Animal Research

All research involving live vertebrate animals must be reviewed and approved by the University’s Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC)prior to the initiation of the research and before a research award can be accepted.

*Note: Laws about data vary outside the U.S.
